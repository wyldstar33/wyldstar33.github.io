LIMINAL FEELINGS

The Dune Sea stretched in all directions before him. The hot sand warmed his skin in a pleasant way as he sat crosslegged on the ground. Around him stood the obelisks — ten of them, in a ring that had no precise geometry, as if they had simply arrived where they needed to be and stayed. Barougian script shone elegantly in the bright Lonestar sun. Each once stood in their respective cities. Here in Ryker’s mind they loomed over him and his life — each a touchpoint, a tulpa of his dreams and nightmares — and the sand beneath him was not empty but full of all the years he had walked it.

He came here when things got loud. A trick he was taught by Roux, a trick to control the Mind Killer he was trained to be.

The Dune Sea, his Dune Sea, was never quiet. Wind and grit and the low subsonic hum of the obelisks always present beneath the silence, but it was the right kind of loud. It was a sound you could think inside of.

He had been doing it less, lately. Coming here. The stones could tell.

OBELISK OF SALVATION i will never be enough and then i will be dead and none of it will have mattered
The Crossroads • Bravado • Lake Bravo

The one that was most his sat slightly apart from the others, white and warm in the sun, and it was the one he visited first because it was the one that felt like breathing. Bravado. The Crossroads. The thing beneath the Crossroads that slept and in sleeping kept something sealed that didn’t deserve to be loosed upon a world that had already paid so much.

He had not understood, for a long time, why Bravado felt like home. He was from Star City. He had wandered. He had bled for the Lucky Ram before he had ever bled for a piece of ground. But this obelisk, when he pressed his palm flat against its face — and he was not embarrassed to admit, here in his own mind, that he did this, that he had stood here with one hand on the stone like a man at a grave — it gave back warmth.

Mammon was here. He still did not know what to do with Mammon. She had been the closest thing to a demon he had ever stood across from, and she had been correct about everything, and she had died for it, and she was still here, waiting at the bottom of a lake in the town he had made himself bleed for. That was not comfortable. That was something he turned over in his hands like a piece of sharp glass, careful of the edges.

But the warmth of this stone was real. Bravado was real. The countless times he had silently helped the Lonestar survive another miserable day were real. He had helped build something here. This obelisk held the proof of it.

He stayed here longest.

OBELISK OF BETRAYAL i have lost too many pieces of myself. who am i. what am i.
Killhouse • Pride’s Hold

This one was close in color to the Salvation stone but the similarity ended there. It was wrong in a way he couldn’t fully articulate — like a note held a half-step flat, like the weight of a room where someone had been angry and left without resolving it.

Killhouse. The Butcher. The Lifers. The days that had no names.

He ran his tongue across his teeth when he looked at this one, an old habit, checking for looseness. They’d hit his face enough times during those months that the action had become reflex. He was not angry about it, particularly. Anger required caring about the person doing the thing. The people in that prison had not merited the cost of his anger, and he had spent it anyway, and that was the truth of those years.

What this obelisk actually held — and he knew this because he had looked, once, in a weak moment — was the smaller things. The names he had given up. The Junkerpunks, spilled from his mouth like water when the Law Dogs pressed hard enough, and the guilt of that living in him still like a splinter he can’t quite reach. The slow fog that came after the experiments, the way certain weeks had gone soft and missing, the paranoia that had not left when the prison gates had. The way he still counted exits in every room he entered. The way he slept facing the door. The way he still trusted Slink, come what may.

He had made his peace with most of it. Made his peace was perhaps the wrong phrase — he had accepted it, which was different and harder. The obelisk was scarred in ways that matched him and he found that oddly appropriate. They had both survived.

OBELISK OF ENTROPY it should have been me. it should have been me. it should have been me.
Star City • Waking

The Star City stone was tall and cold and old-feeling in a way the others were not, and the Barougian script across its face had faded at the edges, worn down by decades of something Ryker could not identify. Not wind. Something internal.

He thought of Jade when he looked at this one. He thought of his mother’s hands. He thought of the specific quality of light at Junior Bootcamp before dawn when the courtyard was still cold and he was fifteen and had not yet understood what the world was going to ask of him.

He thought of the Fallow Hopes in the way you think of a disease you survived — without sentimentality, with a kind of clinical recognition that it had shaped you and that most of what it had shaped was scar tissue.

The obelisk was associated with slow death, with entropy, with the failure of things that once held power. He thought that was apt. Star City had died slowly in him long before whatever was happening to it now.

He did not hate Star City. Hate required investment, and that particular debt had been paid long ago in a courtyard execution that had cost him everything. He touched the obelisk with confidence, and felt a shock of painful energy in return. In a second his body was lit aflame, and then extinguished.

In confusion, he ignored it. He simply had no home there anymore, and this obelisk was the marker where that home had been.

OBELISK OF CREATION where are you mother. why won't you show your face. you made this mess.
New Austen • Essex

The Essex stone was the one he had the most complicated feelings about, and so it was the one he tended to speak to rather than merely observe.

Four was alive. Four had rebuilt himself. Four poured drinks in Essex and had a life that did not require Ryker’s assessment or approval, and Ryker had come to him and they had sat across a table and said the things that needed saying and not all of them had been kind, and afterward the air between them was not clean exactly, but breathable.

The obelisk itself was described, in the old records, as burned out. A husk. The gate it had been was gone, spent in some expenditure too great for it to survive, and what remained was a monument to legacy and nothing more. Ryker found that unbearably precise.

Some things you do not get back. The childhood version of your father. The version of yourself that existed before your little sister was taken. The unguarded trust of someone who had not yet been betrayed by the closest person they had. These were not things that burned out because they failed. They burned out because they were spent on something. The question was whether the something was worth it.

He thought, standing at this stone, that he did not know the answer to that. He thought probably no one ever did, and that was not a tragedy, exactly, but it was a weight.

He had a father. Four was alive. The gate was gone and the stone remained and he had learned to call that enough.

OBELISK OF MEMORY i've never had a handler that didn't betray me. never trust. never sleep.
Widow’s Peak • The Sunken Saints

Assassination was strange to explain even to people who had lived a life of crime, and so Ryker mostly did not explain it. The stone was dark at the base and lighter toward the top, almost like water had sat at it once, and the script here felt — busy. Too much happening at once.

This was the stone of things that would not stay where he put them. Widow’s Peak. Clauthia Lovelace. The tangled and blood-soaked shape of the Widows when they had first formed, when he had trusted them in the way that a man who does not trust easily trusts — completely, in secret, without admitting he was doing it.

The Widows had given him something. He was not sentimental about what they had also taken. That was business. That was the world. He had been the weapon they needed him to be and he had gotten better at his work because of it, and the guilt of who he had become in that period sat in this stone and he visited it when he needed to remember where certain skills had their origin, which was not in righteousness.

Murder Inc had given him a different kind of belonging: the work. There was a version of justice that the law could not execute and he had appointed himself its instrument and he slept fine, mostly, which he took as evidence that his read on the world was accurate.

The Waking Intelligence Service was in here too. Arthur’s forces. Lawrence Graves. The board’s dead-eyed insistence that some people were property, which was the category of wrongness that Ryker could not metabolize no matter how many years passed or how many mouths it came from or in what new forms it would take. He kept coming back to it. The obelisk hummed when he stood near it, subtly, like a tuning fork struck and held.

There were debts here that would never be paid. He knew that. He came to this stone to remember it.

OBELISK OF KNOWLEDGE i know their names, their faces, their routes, their homes. i know too much. i know too much.
Aggieland • Waking

This one made him tired.

He had learned to read in bootcamp. He had learned to read well in the medical corps, where approximation got people killed — where the difference between understanding how Infection moved through a dying Merican body versus a Yorker body was the difference between a patient and a corpse that got back up wrong. The writing had followed the reading. Small crabbed observations in the margins of other people’s notes, accumulating into theories, and then more theories, and then a third theory that contradicted the first two.

The obelisk was dense with script. More than the others. He had spent years feeding it and it had not gotten easier to look at.

He knew things. He knew how Infection moved through a dying brain, which Aberrant behaviors were psionic and which were something else wearing psionic symptoms like a coat, which faith structures were just psychotic cults and where the faith structures touched things with no comfortable name. Not to mention the face and name of every person he had ever met from here to the Iron Harbor and back. The problem was the gaps. Every piece he filled in revealed two more empty spaces around it, and those spaces had shapes — he could see the shapes — but not the pieces that fit them.

He had theories. Too many theories. He had written some down and hidden them and occasionally found them again and thought yes, this is right and occasionally found them again and thought this was written by a man in a bad state. He could not always tell which reading was correct.

He still took notes. He could not stop taking notes.

He stood at this stone and felt something adjacent to exhaustion. He came back to it anyway.

OBELISK OF MIDNIGHT i don't think the balance wants to be kept. it wants to consume until there is nothing left.
The Mortis • The Nightmare Realm

This one was cold. Not unfriendly — just cold, the way certain truths are cold, the way the fact of death is cold not because it is malicious but because it does not care.

The Mortis. The Gravemind. He had been curious about it since Iron Harbor, since the first time he died and came back different in ways he was still cataloguing. Since he had stepped into a Sunless Garden and almost never returned. He was a Reckoner of the Grave Council, a Mind Killer whose life was dedicated to fighting the Damned Fungus beneath their feet. He understood, better than most, what the “Balance” meant in practical terms, what the Nightmare Realm was and what the Khibrath Murea were and how the Infection moved through a body after the primary death and before the tertiary.

He did not come to this stone often. Not out of fear, exactly. More because he was not yet sure what question he was trying to ask, and he had learned — from Slink, from the Grave Council, from the accumulated weight of too many deaths and returns — that you did not stand at a door like this one without knowing what you were going to say.

The obelisk was the heart of the Nightmare Realm. The source of the corruption. Without it the Prince could not have completed his plan to escape through the passage of time itself, and that was the part that sat wrong in Ryker’s chest whenever he thought about it too long — that someone had built something this vast and cold and patient as a plan. As a plan to escape. He understood, maybe more than he should, the desire to escape.

He came to this stone when he needed to remember that the Balance was not metaphor.

OBELISK OF THE TENTACLED DEPTHS i know. i know. i know. i know. i know. i know.
The Clutch • The Spoiled Coast

The Spoiled Coast obelisk smelled like salt and crude oil and he hated that he knew exactly what that combination reminded him of.

The Lucky 7s were in the base of this stone — the crew that had been family before they scattered, the ones who hadn’t followed when the Ram Guard became the Lucky Ram, the ones who stayed out on that slick and rotten coastline and built something of their own. He thought about them sometimes. Not enough. The sediment of time had done what it always did and he was not proud of how rarely those faces surfaced anymore.

But the stone was not really about them. He knew that. He had always known that.

It was about the beach. The waves coming in dark and slow across water the color of an old bruise, the Texas Tea sheening the surface in the last of the evening light. And Abe on the shore, reading. Face locked in concentration, body easy, completely at rest in the way that Ryker had catalogued and could not stop cataloguing — the specific quality of Abe’s stillness, which was the most infuriating thing he had ever encountered and he had encountered a lot.

He could not look at this obelisk straight on. He looked at it the way you look at something bright — peripherally, briefly, with the awareness that looking directly would cost you something.

The water here was getting shallower. That was the thing he had noticed and could not unnotice. The monsters lived at the bottom of the deep, and he knew what he was — he had built himself into something useful and controlled and precise and underneath all of that was the original material, the thing Killhouse had found and the thing the Widows had sharpened, and it was not safe. It was not safe to love and it was not safe to be loved by and the Cicatrix tendrils that wrapped the Spoiled Coast had wrapped him too, and he knew it, and he was terrified of what he would drag under with him when the depth finally ran out.

Abe just wanted to know he wasn’t going to leave. That was all. He had heard it, understood it, filed it in the obelisk where he kept the things he understood and could not act on.

If he said yes to these feelings he would lose the architecture. The careful organized distance that kept everything running. If he said yes and then became what he was afraid of becoming — if the seal cracked, if the control slipped, if the water got too shallow and the things at the bottom came up — Abe would be there. Right there. On that beach.

So he walked past the stone. He noted it. He turned back to look.

He did not sit down.

OBELISK OF ENDLESS SANDS bury me where i stand and let me be where the bloodline ends.
The Dune Sea • The Mind Itself

He was sitting inside this one.

That was the truth of the Dune Sea — not that it was a place he visited, but that it was the medium he moved through, the underlying fact of his interior landscape. The obelisk of the Endless Sands was not one he could stand apart from and observe because it was the ground beneath all the others, the hum under everything, the unease that psions felt and that he had long since learned to wear the way you wore old work clothes — fitting, comfortable in their discomfort, not something you thought about unless someone made you.

Alone in the endless sands. He had heard it said that way and he thought that was the most accurate description of a thing he had struggled to describe. Not lonely — he had plenty of friends and family and enemies and otherwise — but alone in the way that the center of yourself is always alone, the place where the accounting happens, where the things you have done are weighed and the things you are going to do are planned and neither audience nor witness has any standing.

This was where he came from. This was what he was made of. The Dune Sea that imbued Raiders with strange psionic potential, that maintained its own climate against the logic of the world, that kept things alive and harsh and true — that was the substrate of Ryker Stonewall-Wyldstar, and he had made his peace with that long ago, out here where the wind erased footprints and the sun showed no mercy and both of those things were, in the final accounting, fine.

He sat crosslegged in the hot sand and felt the warmth of it.

He did not look at the last stone.

He should look at the last stone.

OBELISK OF THE OPEN PATH you are out of time
Barogue • The Gate of Worlds

He kept his back to it.

He had been keeping his back to it for — he did not want to count the years. It was easier not to. The obelisk was behind him and the sand between his shoulderblades and the back of it was a pressure he was used to, a familiar weight in the way a wound you have carried long enough becomes familiar. You stop noticing it at its sharpest. You only notice when someone asks you to turn around.

The Barougian name for this one was also the Gate of Worlds. The Portus Mundorum. The keystone of the whole network. The first to awaken, the first cracked open, the one that had been weaponized by Tabitha and the Ritual of Indulgence to funnel psionic energy into — and something about that had always sat wrong in Ryker’s chest, the idea that the most central door was the first one to get broken, the idea that the breaking was by design.

He did not tend this stone. He did not visit it. He had put things inside it — all the things that did not fit in the other nine, all the things that were too loud or too sharp or too much — and sealed it, or tried to, and had come to the Dune Sea year after year and sat with his back to the keystone and told himself it was fine.

It was not fine.

He could feel it from here, a tremor in the sand beneath him, faint and rhythmic, like something pacing. Like something aware it was being ignored. The Nightmare Realm was on the other side of this gate and it had always been on the other side of this gate and the rage he had packed inside it — the original rage, the courtyard execution rage, the Jade-shaped hole in his chest that had never closed over right, the names he had given up, the things the Fallow Hope and a dozen others had done to him in the name of making him better, the specific and grinding toll of every year he had spent proving that he was strong enough not to need anything — all of it was in there, pressed against the inside of the seal.

And the seal was cracking.

Not catastrophically. Not yet. But he was not a man who lied to himself about structural integrity, and the structural integrity of the Barogue gate was, currently, not what it had been. The script on the back of it — which he could not see from here, which he made a point of not seeing — was almost certainly showing fracture lines. The keystone was the keystone for a reason. You didn’t neglect the keystone.

He knew this. He had known this for a while. The knowing sat in the hot sand next to him like a companion he had not invited and could not make leave.

The wind turned and brought with it, for just a moment, a sound — not quite a voice, not quite the grinding of stone, something between — and Ryker felt his jaw tighten and his hands still in his lap, the particular stillness of a man deciding whether to acknowledge a thing. He decided, as he always did, to note it and return to it later.

He was not ready to turn around yet.

He would be, though. He would have to be. The Gate of Worlds was the first to awaken in the stories, and Tabitha had cracked the real one open with psionic energy and the machinations of a woman who knew exactly which lock to pick, and Ryker had his own machinations pressing at this door from inside and outside both.

The sun moved. The sand was warm. The nine other obelisks stood in their ring, patient as stones are patient, which is to say absolutely and without judgment.

Behind him, something pressed.
