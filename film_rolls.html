01 Exterior — Entry
A Victorian house at the end of a lot. Unremarkable. Easy to look past.
The ward is visible. A smear of overexposed light sits across the threshold — not a reflection, not a lens flare. It has edges. It was placed there. In frame 14, partially obscured by the door frame: a figure. Not a ghost. Not an intruder. Someone who was admitted. The body language is unhurried. They knew the door would open.
Someone was let in. Recently enough to register. Find out who.

02 Den — Hall Doors
A corridor. Identical doors on both sides. The loop that returns you to the beginning, or somewhere like it.
The doors are not identical. One door frame carries a chalk mark — barely a centimeter, cut into the upper right corner of the jamb. Invisible in ambient light, legible on silver halide. It is a Tremere wayfinding notation, the kind chalked by someone who had to navigate this corridor regularly and couldn't trust their own memory to stay intact. That door is real. The others are the loop.
There is a correct door. Someone who lived here marked it. It leads somewhere specific, and they needed to reach it reliably.

03 Stair Down — Fake Outdoors
A staircase descending into a room that appears to open onto the outside. A sky. Trees, maybe. A sense of open air in a space that should have walls.
The sky is real. Every frame confirms it — not a projection, not a ward-induced hallucination, not a painted ceiling. A sky. The stars in it are not any constellation in the northern hemisphere, or any hemisphere. They are consistent across all thirty-one usable frames. Whatever that room is, it has a sky that was put there. Someone chose those stars specifically and arranged them in that pattern for a reason that has nothing to do with navigation.
That room opens onto somewhere that is not Austin, Texas. The stars are a coordinate, or a key, or a name. They mean something to someone.

04 Scale · Loop · Lux
The distortions. The wrong light. The rooms that refuse proportion. Not the Umbra — something designed, not grown.
The light resolves into structure. What the eye reads as distortion or ambient wrongness has a skeleton — lines of force running through the building's architecture, load-bearing in the magical sense. They intersect at seven points. The film captured five of them clearly. The house's spatial warping does not fill the whole building equally. It is concentrated. It has weight, and the weight rests somewhere specific, and if that point of concentration were disrupted, the geometry would fail.
This is a partial structural map of the warping. The five intersection points are marked. Find the other two and you have the whole blueprint. Find the load-bearing center and you have a way to bring it down.

05 Navarro's Office
The Primogen's office. Papers. A broken lock. Blood on the wall that looked like equations in the moment.
The blood on the wall is not equations. The equations are on the papers. The blood is a circuit schematic — and under flash, on silver halide, it is legible. It diagrams an apparatus that accepts vitae as simultaneous input from multiple collection points and routes it to a central location. The schematic is complete on the visible wall. The wall continues out of frame to the right. The central terminus — where all the blood goes — is not in any of these photographs. Misha's hand was shaking on the last three frames. They're usable anyway.
The circuit is real and it is functional and it routes to somewhere in this building that was never photographed. The schematic is incomplete by exactly one piece: the destination.
